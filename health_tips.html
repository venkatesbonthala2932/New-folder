Food Habits

Healthy Food Habits

By our Nutrition & Internal Medicine team

Eat a rainbow daily. Include at least 5 colours of fruits and vegetables — each colour provides unique phytonutrients and antioxidants your body needs.

Reduce ultra-processed foods. Packaged snacks, instant noodles, and sugary drinks raise your risk of diabetes, heart disease, and obesity. Cook at home when possible.

Choose whole grains. Replace white rice and maida with millets, brown rice, or whole wheat to maintain blood sugar levels and increase fibre intake.

Limit sodium to 5g/day. Excess salt raises blood pressure. Use herbs and spices for flavour instead. Check labels — packaged foods hide salt.

Eat mindfully. Slow down, chew thoroughly, and avoid screens while eating. This improves digestion and prevents overeating.

Physical Activity

Physical Activity Guidance

By our Orthopaedics & Physiotherapy team

150 minutes per week minimum. WHO recommends at least 150 minutes of moderate aerobic activity (brisk walking, cycling, swimming) weekly for adults.

Break sitting time. For every 45-60 minutes of sitting, stand up and walk for 5 minutes. Prolonged sitting raises risk of cardiovascular disease even if you exercise.

Strength training twice a week. Muscle mass declines after age 30. Include bodyweight exercises (squats, push-ups) or resistance bands to preserve strength and bone density.

Warm up and cool down. Always spend 5 minutes warming up before exercise and 5 minutes stretching after to prevent injury and improve flexibility.

Start small, stay consistent. A 20-minute daily walk beats an intense 2-hour weekend session. Consistency is the most important factor in long-term fitness.

Sleep Hygiene

Better Sleep Habits

By our Neurology & Mental Health team

7–9 hours for adults. Chronic sleep deprivation is linked to obesity, diabetes, depression, and weakened immunity. Treat sleep as a health priority.

Keep a fixed sleep schedule. Go to bed and wake up at the same time every day — even on weekends. This regulates your internal body clock.

No screens 1 hour before bed. Blue light from phones and laptops suppresses melatonin production. Use night mode or switch to books in the evening.

Keep your bedroom cool and dark. Optimal sleep temperature is 18–20°C. Use blackout curtains and reduce noise to improve sleep quality.

Avoid caffeine after 2 PM. Caffeine has a half-life of 5–6 hours. An afternoon coffee can still be disrupting your sleep at midnight.

Hydration

Staying Properly Hydrated

By our Nephrology & Internal Medicine team

Drink 2–3 litres of water daily. In Hyderabad's heat, aim for the upper end. Dehydration causes fatigue, headaches, poor concentration, and kidney stones.

Start the day with water. Drink a glass of water first thing in the morning before tea or coffee. It replenishes what you lost overnight.

Check your urine colour. Pale yellow = well hydrated. Dark yellow = drink more water. This is the easiest hydration check you can do.

Eat water-rich foods. Cucumber, watermelon, curd, tomatoes, and oranges all contribute significantly to your daily fluid intake.

Limit sugary drinks. Soft drinks, packaged juices, and energy drinks cause blood sugar spikes and dehydrate you further. Water, buttermilk, and coconut water are best.

Preventive Care

Preventive Health Screenings

By our General Medicine & Diagnostic team

Annual full body checkup after age 30. Many serious conditions — diabetes, hypertension, thyroid issues — show no symptoms early on. A yearly panel catches them before they progress.

Know your numbers. Target: Blood pressure below 120/80, fasting glucose below 100 mg/dL, BMI 18.5–24.9, and total cholesterol below 200 mg/dL.

Stay up to date on vaccinations. Influenza, Hepatitis B, Tetanus, and HPV vaccines are recommended for adults. Consult your doctor for your personal schedule.

Dental checkups every 6 months. Oral health is directly linked to heart health. Gum disease bacteria can enter the bloodstream and increase cardiac risk.

Eye and hearing tests annually. Vision and hearing loss are gradual — you often don't notice. Early correction prevents accidents and improves quality of life significantly.

Stress Management

Managing Stress & Mental Health

By our Psychiatry & Neurology team

Practice deep breathing daily. The 4-7-8 technique (inhale 4s, hold 7s, exhale 8s) activates your parasympathetic nervous system and reduces cortisol within minutes.

Set boundaries with technology. Constant notifications keep your brain in a state of low-level stress. Schedule "phone-free" times — especially during meals and the first 30 minutes after waking.

Maintain social connections. Loneliness is as damaging to health as smoking 15 cigarettes a day. Invest in relationships — call a friend, join a community group, or simply talk to a neighbour.

Try mindfulness or meditation. Even 10 minutes of mindfulness daily reduces anxiety, improves focus, and lowers blood pressure. Apps like Headspace or YouTube guided sessions work well.

Seek help early. Mental health conditions are medical conditions. If you feel persistently low, anxious, or overwhelmed, please speak to a psychiatrist. Treatment is effective and confidential at Aadityaa Hospital.
